I have built a cross-linked database of 22,936 EPA Toxic Release Inventory facilities across all 50 US states, with 155,814 chemical release records, joined to county-level health outcomes and demographic data. The database is free to use, and the data is available for download in journalist-friendly formats (CSV, Excel, JSON).

**Why this matters:**

The EPA publishes facility-level toxic release data through the TRI program, but that data does not include information about the communities living near those facilities. County Health Rankings publishes health outcomes, but not pollution sources. Census data provides demographics, but not environmental context. No free, public tool currently connects all three at national scale. This database does.

**Story angles the data supports:**

- Which communities bear the highest combined burden of toxic chemical releases and poor health outcomes?
- Where are the 44,019 known carcinogen releases (657.5 million pounds total) concentrated, and what do health outcomes look like in those counties?
- Which parent companies are the largest toxic polluters, and in what kinds of communities do they operate?
- State-by-state comparisons: Nevada leads in carcinogen releases (261.7 million lbs), followed by Texas (58.9 million lbs) and Louisiana (45.1 million lbs). What explains these disparities?
- Industry analysis: Which of the 30 industry sectors release the most toxic chemicals, and how does that map to community health?
- How do toxic release patterns differ between high-poverty and low-poverty counties?

**Key data points:**

- 22,936 TRI facilities across all 50 states (Texas leads with 2,084 facilities)
- 155,814 chemical release records covering 571 distinct chemicals
- 6.5 billion total pounds of toxic releases (2022-2023)
- 44,019 carcinogen release records (657.5 million lbs)
- Top chemicals by volume: zinc compounds (1.19 billion lbs), lead and lead compounds (832 million lbs)
- Health outcomes linked for 3,143 US counties
- 4,480 unique parent companies tracked
